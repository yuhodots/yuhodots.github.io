It feels like I just started blogging, yet August is already here. When I first began, I was determined to diligently post about what I studied, but I also worried that after two or three months I'd get bored and my posting frequency would drop drastically. Yet here I am, still writing regularly, which tells me I've been more consistent than I expected.

 Admittedly, four months is quite short compared to how long other bloggers have been running their sites, but since I never thought I'd keep at it this long, by my own standards it counts as a long time. So I'd like to share some personal reflections from my months of blogging. The reason I'm writing this is twofold: first, to encourage people who — like my past self — are on the fence about starting a tech blog to just go ahead and start; and second, so that when I eventually feel too lazy to keep blogging, I can reread this post and find the motivation to keep going. If I'm being honest, the second reason is probably the bigger one.

 In a nutshell, my blog is simply an "online notebook." These days I try to combine what I learn from searching the web with my own studying, and once I understand the material well enough to explain it, I turn it into a post. My earlier posts, however, were nothing more than lecture notes from online courses. Even so, I strongly believe that keeping a blog as a developer is hugely beneficial, for the reasons below. (These are just my personal opinions, so feel free to take them with a grain of salt.)

</br>

#### It provides great motivation.

 When I only kept what I studied in my head without writing it down, I often felt like nothing remained when I looked back later. But once I started posting to my blog, the knowledge accumulated one piece at a time, and seeing the number of posts grow visibly gave me far more satisfaction than before. There were even times when I studied not for the sake of studying itself, but for the sake of having something to post — that's how strongly blogging served as motivation and a driving force for learning. With each new post, I also developed a certain attachment to the blog and resolved never to let it become a ghost blog.

 No matter what goals people set, the "three-day resolution" syndrome tends to kick in — after a few days, the goal is forgotten or abandoned. My blog has done an excellent job of reminding me of my goals and preventing me from getting lazy.

</br>

#### You stop glossing over things you don't fully understand.

 While studying web backend development, one thing I realized is important is that you shouldn't move on to the next step just because your code doesn't throw errors right now. I'm currently past the very basics and studying more advanced backend topics. At this stage, the more I study, the more critical it becomes to have truly understood the earlier material. I'm acutely aware that without a solid understanding of the fundamentals, you can't figure out why your code is throwing errors.

 To write a blog post about something you've studied, even if the material seems simple, you end up reviewing it at least three times to translate your thoughts into written form. Going over the same content multiple times definitely helps you catch things you'd miss on a single pass, and you'll notice a marked increase in your understanding of the material you write about. Ultimately, the very act of explaining something to someone else makes your knowledge more precise and robust.

</br>

#### It actually saves time.

 When working on projects, there are surprisingly many things that are simple but that you just can't remember. Even if you haven't completely forgotten something, situations frequently arise where you need to revisit earlier material to ensure your code is correct. Running a tech blog lets you quickly recall or look up such things. While writing posts does take a significant amount of time, when you factor in the time spent on actual development, in my case blogging has actually allowed me to develop faster.
