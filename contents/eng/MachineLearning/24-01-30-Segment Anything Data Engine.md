> This post reviews the Data Engine and Appendix F sections of Meta AI Research's Segment Anything paper, examining the key considerations in automating the mask annotation process.

### Data Engine

##### 1. Assisted-Manual Stage

In the very first stage, humans manually created mask annotations with partial assistance from the model.

- Initially, SAM was trained on public segmentation datasets.
- Using a browser-based interactive segmentation tool, annotators performed mask annotation by clicking on foreground/background with SAM's assistance. The internal tool included 'brush' and 'eraser' features, making mask refinement straightforward.
- Labels were kept simple as just 'stuff' and 'things,' and annotators were given freedom in how they labeled.
- Once sufficient data was collected, SAM was retrained (this process was repeated 6 times), and the model was scaled up from ViT-B to ViT-H.
- A labeling speed of about 30 seconds per mask was recommended. Initially it took around 34 seconds, which decreased to 14 seconds as the model improved.
- In this stage, a total of 4.3M masks were collected across 120K images.

##### 2. Semi-Automatic Stage

In the next stage, confident masks were pre-generated by the model, and humans annotated only the remaining regions.

- To allow annotators to focus only on less prominent regions, the model pre-generated confident masks, and annotators were asked to annotate only the regions where no masks had been generated.
- For confident mask detection, a bounding box detector with an 'object' class was trained using all mask data created in the first stage. (It would be worthwhile to look into weakly-supervised segmentation methods such as *BoxTeacher* and *SIM* in this context.)
- As in stage (1), the model was retrained (repeated 5 times), and it took an average of about 34 seconds per mask, excluding automatic masks.
- In this stage, a total of 5.9M masks were collected across 180K images (10.2M masks combined with stage 1).

##### 3. Fully Automatic Stage

In the final stage, annotation was fully automated.

- A 32x32 grid of points was fed to the model, and for each point, a mask for the corresponding object was generated.
- The SAM model outputs masks for subparts, parts, and whole objects. Among these, confident masks were selected using the IoU prediction module.
- Only stable masks were then selected: a mask was considered stable if it remained similar when the probability map threshold was shifted to 0.5-a and 0.5+a.
- NMS was applied to the entire set of confident & stable masks to remove duplicates.
- To improve the quality of small masks, techniques such as multiple overlapping zoomed-in image crops were also applied; see Appendix B for details.
- In this stage, a total of 1.1B masks were collected across 11M images.

### Data Annotation Card

##### Task Formulation

- What were the subjective aspects of the task?: Segmentation is inherently subjective. For the same shoe object, one could segment the pair of shoes as a single mask, or segment each shoe individually. Since the focus was on diversity rather than perfection of masks, this ambiguity was acceptable.
- Assumptions related to annotators: Annotators worked full-time, and there was no significant workforce reduction. A clear understanding of the task purpose, well-defined guidelines (visual/video materials), shared OKRs, and weekly meetings with annotators all contributed to increasing the quantity and quality of annotations.
- What was done to verify the clarity of instructions?: The research team directly completed about 30 annotation tasks using the annotation tool to identify complex cases and refine guidelines. Additionally, the research team held weekly feedback sessions with annotators.
- How detailed were the instructions?: Only a high-level instruction was provided: 'For a given image, the goal is to segment all possible objects.' The internal interactive segmentation tool allowed adding or removing masks through foreground/background clicks, drawing bounding boxes, and refining masks with a pixel-precise tool (think of tools like CVAT).

##### Platform and Infrastructure

- Number of annotators: Approximately 130 Kenya-based annotators were involved.
- Platform used for annotation: A proprietary annotation platform was used (interpreted as an internal platform).
- Communication with annotators: Daily feedback was exchanged with the Annotation QA team, and weekly feedback was exchanged with the research team, with regular communication via spreadsheets and chat groups. This process significantly improved both the quantity and quality of data production.
